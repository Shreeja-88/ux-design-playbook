# Nudge Theory
Nudge Theory suggests that people make decisions based on cognitive biases rather than purely rational analysis. It proposes that subtle changes in the way choices are presented can significantly influence people's behavior without restricting their freedom of choice. This involves designing choices that are easier to make or more appealing, guiding users towards desired actions without being overly forceful or restrictive.

Concise summary of the influential behavioral science book, "Nudge", by Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein. The core premise is that because human decision-making is often flawed due to psychological biases, we can design environments—called "nudges"—to gently guide people toward choices that are better for them without restricting their options.

## Key Psychological Biases Explained:
- **Anchoring**: People rely heavily on the first piece of information they receive, such as an initial suggested donation amount, when making decisions.
- **Availability Bias**: We overestimate the likelihood of events if we can easily recall recent examples, like taking out insurance immediately following a natural disaster.
- **Representativeness Heuristic**: The tendency to assume things are more common if they fit a specific stereotype, famously illustrated by the "Linda the Bank Teller" problem.

## How to Design Nudges:
- **Defaults**: Pre-selecting an option (like automatic organ donation enrollment) significantly influences outcomes, as people are more likely to stick with the default.
- **Narrowing the Field**: Reducing overwhelming choices by filtering them (e.g., how Spotify uses filters to help users find music) makes decision-making easier.

--- 

## The Ethics of Nudging:
**Libertarian Paternalism**: The authors argue that since there is no truly neutral way to present choices, designers should intentionally nudge people toward better decisions. This is considered ethical because it does not restrict individual freedom of choice.
